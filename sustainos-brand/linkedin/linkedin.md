# LinkedIn Post Guide — SustainOS

Load this file whenever writing a LinkedIn post for SustainOS or Zyad Hatquai. Follow every section in order: post type, structure, CTA transition rules, then run the end-of-draft checklist before delivering the final post.

---

## Post Types

SustainOS LinkedIn content runs on two post types. Every post is one or the other. Identify the type before writing.

**Meme post** — Built for reach. The meme does the viral work. The caption carries the value and drives conversion. Hook is short and relatable. The point is made in 3-5 short paragraphs. Links are the primary CTA vehicle.

**Value post** — Built for conversion and authority. Longer caption, one clear insight developed fully. Often paired with a one-pager, carousel, or chart as the visual. Can stand alone as text-only when the point is strong enough. Links and follow/subscribe CTAs both matter here.

---

## Post Structure

Every LinkedIn post follows this sequence regardless of type:

**1. Hook (1-3 lines)**
The first thing visible before "see more." This must earn the click. It does one of three things:
- Names a real, specific situation the audience recognizes immediately ("Every sustainability manager I speak to says some version of the same thing: 'I feel behind on AI.'")
- Makes a provocative but honest statement ("Nobody is teaching sustainability professionals how to use AI.")
- Opens a gap the reader needs to close ("Let me be honest with you. No hype. Claude is quietly taking over.")

Do not open with a question. Do not open with "In today's post." Do not open with a generic observation.

**2. Body (4-8 short paragraphs)**
Develop the one point. Use short sentences. One idea per paragraph. Generous line breaks. No walls of text.

Use real specifics: tool names, regulation names, real numbers, concrete outcomes. Vague claims get ignored. Specific ones get shared.

The body must earn the CTA. If the reader does not feel the point by the end of the body, the CTA will feel bolted on.

**3. CTA transition sentence (1-2 lines)**
This is the most important writing move in the post. The CTA cannot appear without a transition. The transition connects what was just said directly to the resource being linked. It does not announce "here are my links." It grows naturally from the point that was made.

See the CTA Transition Rules section below for patterns and examples.

**4. Links or follow instruction**
After the transition, add the links or follow instruction. Multiple links perform better than one. Each link gets a short label.

---

## CTA Transition Rules

The transition sentence is not a separator. It is the logical next step from the post's main point to the resource. Write it last, after the body is done, so it responds to what was actually said rather than being templated.

**The test:** Read the last paragraph of the body, then read the transition. If the transition could be copy-pasted onto a different post without changing a word, it is too generic. Rewrite it.

**Patterns that work:**

*If the post is about a specific workflow or tool:*
Connect the transition to the outcome the reader just learned about.
"This is exactly what I have been applying to real sustainability workflows and documenting as I go."

*If the post is about a mindset shift or insight:*
Connect the transition to the place where the reader can go deeper.
"If this landed, you will find the rest useful too." (from real post)
"Every week in the SustainOS newsletter I share exactly this kind of thinking." (from real post)

*If the post is about a problem the audience has:*
Connect the transition to the solution the reader can access now.
"I have been building the resource library sustainability pros actually need. Starting here."

*If the post is a meme post:*
Keep the transition brief. The meme already did the reach work. Get to the value fast.
"If you are in the apply and share camp like me, here are real workflows I have been testing."

**What to avoid:**
- "Check out my links below." (announces, does not connect)
- "Follow me for more." placed without a preceding transition sentence. (feels like a tag-on)
- Repeating the post's thesis word for word in the transition. (redundant)
- Starting the transition with "Also," or "By the way,". (kills momentum)

## End-of-Draft Checklist

After the post body and transition are written, stop and run through these three questions before delivering the final draft. Ask Zyad each one in sequence.

**Question 1: Workflow links**
"Do you want to add workflow links to this post? If yes, paste the links and labels you want to use, or tell me which workflows to reference and I will write the labels."

When links are added, place them after the transition sentence, each on its own line with a short label. Do not number them. Example format:
```
Build ESG Tools with AI - [link]
AI for complete audit automation - [link]
AI that takes workload off your desk - [link]
```

**Question 2: Self-tag**
"Do you want to tag yourself as Zyad Hatquai in the follow CTA?"

If yes, the follow line becomes: "Follow Zyad Hatquai for [relevant description]"
If no, use "Follow me for [relevant description]" or omit the follow line entirely if links are sufficient.

**Question 3: Newsletter CTA**
"Do you want to add a newsletter CTA? If yes, share the link for this issue."

If yes, the newsletter CTA must also follow a transition, not just appear as a line. Connect it to what the post was about.
Example: "I go deeper on this every two weeks in the SustainOS newsletter. If this was useful, the rest will be too: [link]"

Do not add the newsletter CTA as a standalone line without a connecting sentence.

## Voice Reminders for LinkedIn Specifically

- Emoji are acceptable but not mandatory. One or two per post maximum. Use them as punctuation (at the end of a line), not decoration scattered through the text.
- Bold text is not available in LinkedIn body copy. Write for impact through word choice and structure, not formatting.
- The hook must work without the visual. The visual should add to it, not complete it.
- Hashtags: add 3-5 at the very end if needed, but they are not required. Never mid-post.
- Line breaks are structural. A new idea gets a new line. A new paragraph gets a blank line between it and the previous one.
